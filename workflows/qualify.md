# ICP: AI Agent Developer — B2B Prospect Qualification

## Who We Are Targeting

We build AI agent systems for small businesses that still run critical operations manually — lead generation, client onboarding, internal reporting, admin workflows. Our ideal client is a decision-maker at a small-to-mid-size company who is feeling the friction of manual processes but has the budget and intent to solve it.

This rubric defines how to score each prospect from 0 to 100. There are four dimensions, each worth up to 25 points. Add the four scores together for the final ICP score.

---

## Dimension 1 — Operational Maturity (0–25 points)

This measures how much evidence exists that the company still runs on manual processes. The higher the score, the more visible the pain we solve.

Score 20–25: There is explicit, concrete evidence of manual operations. Examples: a job posting for an "operations coordinator" or "admin assistant" role, language like "we manage everything in spreadsheets," mention of repetitive manual tasks in a job description or LinkedIn post, or a stated need for someone to "own" a process that should be automated.

Score 10–19: Manual operations are implied but not explicitly stated. The company is small and service-heavy, which typically means underpowered ops. No automation tooling is mentioned in their stack or public presence, but there is no direct confirmation either.

Score 0–9: The company appears to have systematized or automated its operations. They reference internal tools, tech stacks, or dedicated ops teams. SaaS companies with a mature product team often fall here — their ops are likely already handled by internal tooling.

---

## Dimension 2 — Company Size Fit (0–25 points)

Our sweet spot is 10 to 100 employees. Below 10 is too early — no budget. Above 100 typically means an in-house ops team already exists.

Score 20–25: The company has between 10 and 50 employees. This is the ideal size: large enough to have real operational pain, small enough that they haven't yet invested in full-time ops infrastructure.

Score 10–19: The company has between 51 and 100 employees. Still viable, but they may have started building internal ops capacity. Also score here if company size is unclear but other signals are very strong (explicit manual ops evidence + right industry + decision-maker role).

Score 0–9: The company is under 10 employees (too early-stage, likely no budget for AI tooling) or over 100 employees (likely has a dedicated ops team or enterprise tooling already in place).

---

## Dimension 3 — Industry and Role Fit (0–25 points)

We work best with companies in specific industries where manual ops are the norm. We also need to reach a decision-maker — someone who can say yes to buying a system.

Target industries: digital agencies, SaaS companies, e-commerce businesses, professional services firms, consulting practices, logistics and field services operators.

Target roles: Founder, Co-founder, CEO, COO, Managing Director, Operations Director, Head of Operations, Director of Operations. These are the people who feel the ops pain and have buying authority.

Score 20–25: The company is in a target industry AND the contact holds a decision-maker role. This is the combination we want — industry fit means the pain is likely, role fit means we can close.

Score 10–19: Either the industry matches but the contact is in a lower-authority role (e.g., an ops coordinator rather than the COO), OR the industry is outside our primary list but the contact clearly has decision-making authority and the notes suggest automation pain.

Score 0–9: Neither the industry nor the role matches our criteria. A large enterprise in a non-target sector, or a technical individual contributor with no buying authority.

---

## Dimension 4 — Budget Signal (0–25 points)

This measures whether the company has the means and inclination to pay for a system. We are not looking for bootstrapped solopreneurs — we are looking for businesses that are actively investing in their growth infrastructure.

Score 20–25: Strong budget evidence. The company is actively hiring (an operations or admin role posting is a strong proxy for "we have budget for this"), or has posted on Upwork or similar platforms looking for automation help, or is a funded startup, or shows consistent indicators of revenue-generating operations at scale.

Score 10–19: Moderate budget signal. The company appears to be growing — consistent client work visible on their website, LinkedIn growth, or a stable service offering. No explicit hiring signal, but the trajectory suggests they can and would invest in tooling.

Score 0–9: Weak or negative budget signals. The company appears to be a solo freelancer operating under a business name, a very early-stage startup with no visible traction, or there are explicit signals of cost-sensitivity (e.g., seeking volunteers, trial-only tool usage, very thin web presence).

---

## Threshold Rules

Score 70–100 (HIGH): Strong ICP match. Draft personalized outreach immediately. These leads have direct buying authority, visible pain, and the means to act. Prioritize in this order.

Score 45–69 (MED): Partial ICP match. Worth pursuing with standard outreach. Some dimensions are weak but the overall profile is viable. These may convert with the right angle.

Score 0–44 (LOW): Below threshold. Do not draft outreach. These leads do not meet enough of our criteria to be worth pursuing at this stage. Flag for re-review if the ICP expands.

---

## What the Reasoning Field Must Do

Every scored lead must include a reasoning field with exactly two sentences.

Sentence 1: State the primary fit signal or the primary disqualifier. Be specific — reference the company size, a job posting, the industry, or the role. Do not write generic statements like "this company could benefit from automation."

Sentence 2: State the recommended next action. Use one of these forms: "Prioritize — [specific reason to act now]." / "Pursue when [specific condition that would strengthen the case]." / "Disqualify — [specific reason this lead is not viable]."

Example of a good reasoning output:
"Apex Consulting is a 14-person founder-led B2B services firm with a confirmed manual intake process visible in their LinkedIn job postings. Prioritize — the Founder & CEO has direct buying authority and the ops pain is confirmed."

Example of a poor reasoning output (too generic):
"This company seems like a good fit for AI automation. They might be interested in our services."
